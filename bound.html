Codex Essay

adjective · past participle · Old English

bound

/baʊnd/

Meaning I.

Heading toward; on one’s way to a place or outcome.

“The freighter was bound for Lisbon, its hull low in the water.”

“She felt certain she was bound for something larger than the town.”

vs
Meaning II.

Restrained; unable to move or act freely; tied.

“His hands were bound behind him with coarse rope.”

“She felt bound to the house by obligation she could not name.”

Etymology

Two entirely separate Old English words collapse into the same modern form. Bound in the sense of heading toward descends from Old Norse búinn — past participle of búa, “to prepare, to make ready” — arriving in Middle English via Scandinavian-settled northern dialects. The terminal -d was added by false analogy with past participles.

Bound in the sense of restrained is the past participle of bind, from Old English bindan, Proto-Germanic *bindaną. The two lineages diverged a millennium ago and reunited in spelling only by accident of phonological drift.

Usage in sentences

  • Toward Every train in the terminal was bound for somewhere he had never been.
  • Toward Duty-bound, he returned to the city he had fled twice before.
  • Toward The probe is bound for the outer planets; it will not return.
  • Restrained The pages were bound in calfskin, the text locked away inside.
  • Restrained He was contractually bound to silence for seven years.
  • Restrained The wound was bound tightly; he would not bleed again tonight.

The tension

What makes bound particularly uncanny among contranyms is that its two senses are not merely opposite in meaning — they describe opposite relationships to movement itself. One bound is pure momentum, directionality, the arrow of intent. The other is the negation of movement, the condition of being held.

And yet the word is the same. Say it aloud — bound — and nothing in the sound tells you which it is. Context does all the work. Strip the context and the word becomes genuinely ambiguous: She was bound. Going somewhere? Or prevented from going anywhere?

Both, perhaps. The English language occasionally achieves accidental poetry.
